## The Color Wheel

The color wheel is a visual representation of color relationships. Understanding it is fundamental to color theory.

### Primary Colors
- **Red**, **Yellow**, **Blue** (traditional)
- Form the basis of all other colors

### Secondary Colors
- Created by mixing two primary colors
- **Orange** (Red + Yellow), **Green** (Yellow + Blue), **Violet** (Blue + Red)

### Tertiary Colors
- Created by mixing a primary and secondary color
- Examples: Red-Orange, Yellow-Green, Blue-Violet

## Color Models

### RGB (Red, Green, Blue)
- **Used for**: Screens, digital displays, web
- **Range**: 0-255 for each color
- **Example**: rgb(255, 0, 0) = Red
- **When to use**: Web design, digital interfaces

### HSL (Hue, Saturation, Lightness)
- **Hue**: Position on the color wheel (0-360°)
- **Saturation**: Intensity of the color (0-100%)
- **Lightness**: Brightness of the color (0-100%)
- **Example**: hsl(0, 100%, 50%) = Pure Red
- **When to use**: Creating color variations and harmonies

### HEX (Hexadecimal)
- **Used for**: Web and digital design
- **Format**: #RRGGBB
- **Example**: #FF0000 = Red
- **When to use**: CSS, web design

### CMYK (Cyan, Magenta, Yellow, Key/Black)
- **Used for**: Print design
- **Range**: 0-100% for each color
- **When to use**: Printing, marketing materials

## Color Harmony

### 1. Complementary
- **Colors opposite on the wheel**
- **Visual impact**: High contrast, energetic
- **Use case**: Call-to-action buttons, highlights
- **Example**: Blue & Orange, Red & Green

### 2. Analogous
- **Colors adjacent on the wheel**
- **Visual impact**: Harmonious, soothing
- **Use case**: Full designs, cohesive palettes
- **Example**: Blue, Blue-Green, Green

### 3. Triadic
- **Three colors equally spaced on the wheel**
- **Visual impact**: Balanced, vibrant
- **Use case**: Creative designs, playful interfaces

### 4. Tetradic (Square)
- **Four colors equally spaced**
- **Visual impact**: Rich, complex
- **Use case**: Sophisticated, diverse designs

### 5. Split-Complementary
- **One color + two colors adjacent to its complement**
- **Visual impact**: Less tension than complementary
- **Use case**: Balanced, interesting palettes

## Creating Palettes

### Step 1: Choose a Base Color
- Pick the color that represents your brand
- This becomes your primary color

### Step 2: Select a Harmony Method
- Use complementary for high contrast
- Use analogous for harmony
- Use triadic for balance

### Step 3: Define Variations
- **Tints**: Add white (lighter versions)
- **Shades**: Add black (darker versions)
- **Tones**: Add gray (muted versions)

### Step 4: Add Neutral Colors
- Add grays for backgrounds and text
- Ensure sufficient contrast for readability

### Example Palette
```
Primary:        #0066CC (Blue)
Secondary:      #FF6600 (Orange) - Complementary

Neutral Colors:
- Dark Gray:    #333333
- Light Gray:   #F5F5F5
- White:        #FFFFFF
- Black:        #000000
```

- **Khroma.co**: AI that learns your preferences

## Accessibility & Contrast

### WCAG Contrast Requirements
- **Normal text**: Minimum 4.5:1 contrast ratio
- **Large text**: Minimum 3:1 contrast ratio
- **Graphics/UI**: Minimum 3:1 contrast ratio

### Best Practices
- ✅ Never rely solely on color to communicate
- ✅ Use patterns, textures, or labels alongside color
- ✅ Test with color blindness simulators
- ✅ Ensure sufficient contrast ratios

## Practical Exercises

### Exercise 1: Color Wheel Analysis
1. Find your favorite website or app
2. Extract the colors used
3. Plot them on a color wheel
4. Identify the harmony method used
5. Document your findings

### Exercise 2: Create a 5-Color Palette
1. Choose a primary color
2. Select a harmony method
3. Create tints and shades
4. Add neutral grays
5. Export as a reference document

### Exercise 3: Contrast Testing
1. Create 3 text/background combinations
2. Test contrast ratios using WebAIM
3. Make adjustments for accessibility
4. Document the before/after

### Exercise 4: Color Psychology Analysis
1. Choose an industry (tech, healthcare, fashion)
2. Analyze 5 brands in that industry
3. Document the colors they use
4. Explain why those colors work

### Exercise 5: Create a Brand Palette
1. Define a fictional brand
2. Choose colors matching brand personality
3. Create a 6-10 color palette
4. Show usage in different contexts
5. Justify your choices

## Key Takeaways

✅ **Understand the color wheel** - Foundational to all color work

✅ **Consider psychology** - Colors trigger emotions
✅ **Prioritize accessibility** - Ensure contrast and don't rely solely on color
